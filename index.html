Plant Health Frequently Asked Questions

Yellow leaves can be caused by several things:

  • Overwatering: The most common cause. Roots need oxygen, and constantly wet soil suffocates them. Let the top inch or two of soil dry out between waterings.
  • Underwatering: If the plant is very dry, lower leaves might yellow and drop.
  • Nutrient Deficiency: Especially nitrogen deficiency can cause overall yellowing, often starting with older leaves. Consider using a balanced liquid fertilizer during the growing season.
  • Lighting Issues: Too much or too little light can stress a plant. Check the specific needs of your plant.
  • Pests: Pests like spider mites can cause yellow stippling.

Brown, crispy tips usually indicate an issue with watering or humidity:

  • Low Humidity: Many houseplants are tropical and prefer higher humidity than average homes provide. Try grouping plants, using a pebble tray with water, or running a humidifier.
  • Inconsistent Watering: Letting the soil get bone dry before watering can stress the plant.
  • Water Quality: Tap water can contain minerals (fluoride, chlorine, salts) that build up in the soil and burn leaf tips. Try using filtered, distilled, or rainwater.
  • Over-fertilizing: Excess fertilizer salts can also burn the roots and leaf tips. Flush the soil with water if you suspect this.

Drooping is often a sign of watering issues:

  • Underwatering: The most obvious cause. If the soil is dry, give the plant a thorough watering. It should perk up within a few hours.
  • Overwatering: Ironically, overwatering can also cause wilting because damaged roots can't absorb water effectively. Check the soil moisture. If it's soggy, let it dry out significantly before watering again. Ensure good drainage.
  • Transplant Shock: Recently repotted plants might wilt temporarily as they adjust. Keep them out of direct sun and water appropriately.
  • Temperature Stress: Extreme heat or cold drafts can cause wilting.

Pest control requires persistence:

  • Fungus Gnats: These tiny flies thrive in moist soil. Let the topsoil dry out more between waterings. Use yellow sticky traps to catch adults. Consider a soil drench with diluted hydrogen peroxide or beneficial nematodes.
  • Spider Mites: Look for fine webbing and tiny dots (the mites) on leaves, especially undersides. They hate moisture. Regularly wipe leaves (top and bottom) with a damp cloth. Increase humidity. Use insecticidal soap or neem oil, ensuring full coverage and repeat applications.
  • Mealybugs/Scale: Dab individual pests with rubbing alcohol on a cotton swab. For larger infestations, use insecticidal soap or neem oil.
  • Isolate Infected Plants: Always isolate affected plants immediately to prevent pests from spreading.

Root rot is a serious condition caused by overwatering and poor drainage, leading to fungal growth.

  • Signs: Yellowing/dropping leaves (often lower ones first), wilting even when soil is moist, stunted growth, mushy stems near the base, a foul smell from the soil.
  • Treatment: 1. Gently remove the plant from its pot. 2. Wash away the old soil to inspect the roots. 3. Trim away any brown, mushy, or smelly roots with sterilized scissors, leaving only healthy white/firm roots. 4. If significant root loss occurred, prune some foliage to reduce stress. 5. Repot in fresh, well-draining potting mix and a clean pot (preferably terracotta to help wick moisture) with drainage holes. 6. Water sparingly initially, allowing the soil to dry more than usual.
  • Prognosis: Survival depends on how much healthy root system remains. Early detection is key.
